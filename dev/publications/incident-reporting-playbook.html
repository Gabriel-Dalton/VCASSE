Incident Reporting Playbook for Responsible AI Teams

A simple operational playbook for classifying incidents, escalating quickly, and learning from postmortems.

  • PublishedDec 12, 2025
  • Reading5 min
  • AuthorsPriya Sandhu

Most AI-related incidents are detected by someone who isn't on the team that built the model. The bottleneck is rarely detection — it's escalation. Without a shared protocol, hours pass between a problem being noticed and the right people hearing about it.

Three layers of severity

This playbook proposes three response tiers:

  • Tier 1 — observable issue: model output looks wrong, but no one is harmed yet. Document, monitor, schedule fix.
  • Tier 2 — user impact: a person receives a wrong, biased, or harmful output. Page the on-call owner; mitigate within hours.
  • Tier 3 — systemic harm: the issue affects a protected group, a regulated process, or public safety. Page leadership; consider taking the system offline.

Ownership and escalation windows

Each tier has a named owner and a time-boxed escalation window. The owner is accountable for both the technical fix and the public-facing communication. We recommend that public-sector teams publish their on-call rota internally so non-engineers know who to reach when something goes wrong.

Postmortems that change behaviour

A postmortem is only valuable if it changes the next deployment. VCASSE recommends documenting four things every time: what happened, who was affected, what we will change in the system, and what we will change in our process. The fourth is the one most teams skip — and it's the one that compounds.

"The point of a playbook is not to predict every failure. It's to make sure that when one happens, the response is boring."

Key takeaways

  • Adopt three explicit severity tiers with named owners.
  • Set escalation windows in hours, not "as soon as possible".
  • Page leadership when harm crosses into protected groups or regulated processes.
  • Postmortems must change the next deployment, not just the team's notes.
