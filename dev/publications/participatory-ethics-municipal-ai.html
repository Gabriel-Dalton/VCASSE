Participatory Ethics in Municipal AI Decisions

Why public-facing AI decisions should include structured resident participation, not only internal review.

  • PublishedJan 31, 2026
  • Reading8 min
  • AuthorsDr. Amara Okafor, Jordan Blackwood

Ethics reviews are strongest when affected communities can shape the criteria and the outcome — not when they are asked to react to a decision that has already been made. Most municipal AI projects today still treat residents as the audience for a final answer, rather than as participants in defining the question.

Three concrete practices

  1. Publish a use-case brief before procurement. A two-page plain-language document describing the problem, the proposed system, and the populations most affected.
  2. Run targeted listening sessions. Invite the populations most likely to encounter the system — not only the public-at-large — and document what changes as a result.
  3. Maintain a deliberation trail. Make it easy for residents to see what feedback was received and how it influenced the deployment, including where it was overruled and why.

Participation is not the same as polling

A survey can tell you what a population thinks about a system in the abstract. Participation tells you what changes when people sit with the actual decision: which language coverage matters, which appeals process works, which communications are trusted. These are findings that don't show up in a Likert scale.

What this looks like in Vancouver

VCASSE is piloting this approach with two municipal partners in 2026. Early findings: structured listening surfaced accessibility considerations that internal review had missed entirely, and changed the eligibility logic of one decision-support tool before it shipped.

"Residents are not the audience for a public-sector AI decision. They are co-authors of it."

Practices to adopt

  • Publish a plain-language use-case brief before procurement.
  • Run targeted listening sessions with affected populations.
  • Maintain a public deliberation trail showing how feedback shaped decisions.
  • Treat residents as co-authors, not respondents.
