Ethics

Participatory Ethics Reviews for Civic AI

Bringing residents, frontline staff, and external reviewers into AI ethics processes — without slowing them to a halt.

  • PublishedFeb 22, 2026
  • Reading10 min
  • AuthorsDr. Aisha Khan, Tomás Rivera

Most municipal ethics reviews for AI live in a small room. A handful of senior staff, occasionally a legal counsel, sometimes one external reviewer who is paid by the same agency they are reviewing. The output is usually a memo, the memo is rarely read, and the system ships either way. This is not an ethics process. It is a paperwork process with an ethics label.

Participatory ethics review is not the opposite of speed. Done well, it is faster than a tribunal-style review, because it surfaces the disagreements that would have shown up after launch, while there is still time to act on them.

What participation actually adds

Three things, in order of how often we see them missed.

  1. Lived context. Frontline staff and residents catch failure modes that look fine on paper. A "polite refusal" message that reads as condescending in Cantonese. A queue position that makes sense to a designer and not to anyone who has waited in one.
  2. Standing to disagree. A reviewer paid by the agency under review will hesitate before writing the sharp sentence. An external reviewer with a clear mandate will not. The institution gets the disagreement it needs, before the press does.
  3. A shared record. Participatory reviews leave behind a document that residents and council members can engage with. The internal-only review leaves behind a document nobody outside the building can quote.
The fastest way to slow an ethics process down is to keep it small. Disagreements that are not surfaced get surfaced later, by people who do not have to be polite about it.

A workable review structure

The reviews we have seen succeed share a structure. It is not the only structure that works, but it is the one we recommend to teams that have not run a participatory review before.

  • A standing panel of five to seven members, including at least two residents recruited through community partners, at least one frontline staff member from a different department, and at least one external reviewer with no contractual relationship to the deploying agency.
  • A short, predictable charter — what the panel reviews, what it does not, and what authority its findings carry. Ambiguity here is the single most reliable predictor of a process that collapses by month four.
  • A single-page brief for each system under review, written by the deploying team, in plain language. If the brief cannot be written in plain language, the system is not ready for review.
  • A published response from the deploying team to each finding, posted alongside the finding. Silent disagreement erodes the process; written disagreement strengthens it.

Three patterns we see go wrong

  1. The advisory panel with no teeth. Findings go in, nothing comes out. After two cycles, the residents stop showing up.
  2. The review held after the contract is signed. By the time the panel sees the system, there is no decision left to make. The review becomes a rubber stamp.
  3. The expert-only panel. A roomful of credentials but no lived context. The findings sound rigorous and miss the failure modes that actually show up in production.
We didn't lose the public's trust because we made a bad call. We lost it because we made it in a room they weren't allowed into. Municipal communications lead, anonymized, 2025

How to compensate participants

Pay residents for their time, in cash, at a rate that reflects what the agency would pay an external consultant for equivalent hours. Honoraria of $25 for a three-hour review are how participatory processes reproduce the inequities they are meant to surface. The budget line for participation should be a real budget line, not a discretionary fund the chair has to defend each year.

Where this connects to the rest of the work

Ethics review is not a substitute for safety evaluation, and neither is a substitute for sustainability disclosure. The three are independent, and they fail differently when collapsed into a single process. Teams that try to bundle them tend to produce a document that satisfies none of the three audiences. Teams that keep them separate, but coordinated on cadence, produce documents that each audience can actually use.

What we are working on next

The next note in this series will cover how participatory reviews intersect with FOI requests and what to do when a finding becomes part of a public records request. If your team is standing up a panel and would like to compare charters, our contact form is the right place to start.

Key takeaways

  • Run reviews with a standing panel that includes residents and frontline staff.
  • Write a short charter that names the panel's authority and limits.
  • Publish the deploying team's written response alongside each finding.
  • Pay participants at consultant-equivalent rates, from a real budget line.
