Energy & Water Footprints of Frontier AI: A Vancouver Procurement Lens

What public buyers should ask vendors about energy, water, and embodied carbon before signing an AI contract.

  • PublishedApr 8, 2026
  • Reading9 min
  • AuthorsDr. Léa Moreau, Jordan Park

Most public-sector AI contracts in BC are still signed without a single number on energy use, water draw, or embodied carbon. That is a procurement problem before it is an environmental one — buyers are comparing offers without a comparable unit.

This brief is for procurement officers, sustainability leads, and elected officials who have to decide whether a particular AI deployment is consistent with municipal and provincial climate commitments. It is not a life-cycle assessment. It is a checklist of the questions a buyer can reasonably ask, and the answers that should make a buyer pause.

The four numbers worth asking for

Vendor disclosures vary wildly. Some publish global aggregates, some publish nothing, and a small number publish per-query estimates that are difficult to verify. For most procurement decisions, four numbers are enough to start a useful conversation.

  1. Inference energy per 1,000 requests, in kilowatt-hours, for the specific model variant you intend to deploy. Not the flagship model. Not last year's model. The one in the contract.
  2. Cooling water draw at the data centres serving Canadian customers, including any evaporative loss, expressed per 1,000 requests if available, or as a regional total with a denominator if not.
  3. Grid carbon intensity at the serving region, hourly if possible, with a clear pointer to which region's grid actually handles your workload.
  4. Embodied emissions per accelerator, amortised over a stated useful-life assumption, so that buyers can reason about the share attributable to their workload over the contract term.
A vendor that cannot produce these four numbers is not unable to. They have decided not to. VCASSE procurement workshop, 2025

What a credible disclosure looks like

Credibility is mostly about denominators. A glossy "carbon-neutral cloud" claim with no underlying request volume, no regional breakdown, and no audited boundary is the procurement equivalent of "trust us." A credible disclosure ties each number to a measurable unit of work, names the third party that verified it, and is willing to be cited in a council document by name.

  • Boundary stated. Does the figure cover training, inference, idle baseline, or some combination? If the boundary is not stated, the number is not comparable.
  • Region named. A provincial or state-level region, not a continent. Grid carbon intensity in Quebec and Wyoming differ by more than an order of magnitude.
  • Verification party named. Internal claims are fine as a starting point, but procurement-grade disclosures should be reviewable by a third party the buyer can contact directly.
  • Update cadence stated. A 2022 disclosure for a 2026 model is not a disclosure. Ask when the figure was last refreshed and what triggers a refresh.

Where buyers usually go wrong

Across the procurements VCASSE has reviewed, a small number of recurring mistakes account for most of the gap between intent and outcome.

  1. Accepting a global average. A worldwide kWh-per-query figure tells you almost nothing about the specific deployment that will serve Vancouver users.
  2. Ignoring water entirely. Water is often the binding constraint at hyperscale data centres before energy is. It rarely shows up in standard procurement templates.
  3. Treating offsets as equivalent to reductions. Offsets are easier to procure and harder to verify. They are not a substitute for a measured reduction.
  4. Locking in long contracts without a refresh clause. Frontier AI infrastructure is changing faster than five-year procurement cycles. Buyers should retain the right to revisit disclosures annually.
Sustainability claims that cannot survive being read aloud at a council meeting probably should not survive procurement.

A short procurement clause that works

The most useful single artefact we have seen is a short procurement clause that requires the vendor to disclose, on request and in writing, the four numbers above for the specific deployment under contract. The clause does not specify thresholds — that is for council to decide — but it makes it impossible to sign without those numbers being on the table. We are happy to share a template version with any BC public-sector buyer who asks.

What we are working on next

The next note in this series will cover regional grid signalling and how municipalities can route AI workloads to lower-carbon hours without paying a vendor premium for the privilege. If your team is preparing a procurement and would like to compare drafts, our contact form is the place to start.

Key takeaways

  • Ask for inference energy, water draw, grid intensity, and embodied emissions per accelerator.
  • Reject global averages — insist on the deployment region you will actually use.
  • Treat offsets as a separate question from measured reductions.
  • Build an annual disclosure-refresh clause into multi-year contracts.
